Breeding intelligence

Every breeding starts
with one decision.

Equine breeding falls under two categories. Understanding which path is right for your mare, your goals, and your budget is the foundation of every successful breeding program.

Conventional breeding with Artificial Insemination (AI)

The right starting point for most breeders.

Conventional breeding via Artificial Insemination (AI) is recommended for first-time breeders, recreational breeders, and anyone whose mare does not have significant competition earnings or bloodline investment. The process follows equine physiology directly and requires no specialized clinic infrastructure beyond a competent reproductive vet.

Cooled

Collected and chilled, shipped overnight in a temp-controlled container. Used within 24–48 hours of collection. Most flexible for distance breeding.

Fresh

Same-day, on-site collection and insemination. Highest conception rate per dose. Mare and stallion must be at the same facility, or the mare ships in.

Frozen

Stored indefinitely in liquid nitrogen. Used after a stallion's death or when shipping fresh isn't viable. Lower conception rate per dose, requires precise ovulation timing.

Select a stallion and pick the semen format above (or live cover if the mare and stallion are at the same facility). Synchronize the mare to pre-ovulatory status using hormone protocols, inseminate, and confirm via ultrasound at day 7 and again at day 11 or 12 to verify a developing embryo.

If the embryo is developing normally, the mare carries the foal for approximately 11 months. She will be off competition for the duration of the pregnancy.

When conventional is the right call
—First-time breeder learning the process
—Mare without documented competition earnings
—Recreational or single-foal goal
—Budget under $3,000 all-in
—No urgency to return the mare to competition
Advanced reproductive technologies

For breeders protecting elite genetic investment.

ART is for breeders who have made a significant investment in a mare, whether in purchase price, competition earnings, or bloodline value. The core question is this: if your mare has documented earnings and a proven genetic record, is it worth having her carry an 11-month pregnancy and sit out a full show season? Most serious breeders answer no. They flush an embryo, place it in a recipient mare, and return the donor to competition the following cycle.

ART methods include embryo flushing (most common), intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI), and ovum pick-up (OPU) for mares that do not flush well. Each method is selected based on the mare's reproductive history, age, and the volume of embryos desired. A certified ET clinic manages the flush, the recipient synchronization, and the transfer procedure.

Embryos can be transferred fresh into a synchronized recipient, frozen for later use or sale, or banked as genetic insurance. Breeders frequently sell embryos outright to recoup the cost of the program. ART also allows breeders to plan ahead by flushing mares in late December or January, transferring embryos into recipients, and producing foals that are born earlier in the year than conventionally bred foals, which can be a competitive advantage in age-class events where early January birthdays matter.

When ART is the right call
—Mare with documented earnings or competition record
—Breeder wants multiple foals from one mare in a season
—Mare needs to remain in active competition
—Goal is to sell embryos and recoup investment
—Mare has passed and ovaries can be harvested to continue her bloodline
—Breeder wants foals born early in the calendar year

The ART pipeline · embryo transfer

Your champion mare,
never out of the arena.

When ART is the right path, embryo transfer is the most established route. Through a partner ET clinic, we connect you to the full pipeline — from donor mare flush to surrogate placement. Your mare keeps competing. Her performance genetics keep producing.

1
Donor mare is synchronized and flushed

The donor mare is hormonally synchronized, bred with selected stallion semen, and flushed 7–8 days later to collect the embryo. One mare can produce 4–8 viable embryos per year.

2
Embryo is graded and stored

Each embryo is evaluated under microscope, graded for quality, and either transferred fresh to a surrogate or cryopreserved for future use. Frozen embryos can be shipped worldwide.

3
Surrogate mare carries to term

A recipient mare (the surrogate) of similar size is synchronized and receives the embryo. New to ET? Here's the foundational fact every breeder should understand: the foal is 100% genetically the donor mare and the chosen stallion. The surrogate carries the pregnancy, but contributes zero genetics. She is the safe vehicle, not the genetic mother — her bloodline, color, and conformation do not appear in the foal.

4
AQHA registration and delivery

ET foals are fully registerable with the AQHA. We coordinate all paperwork, bloodtyping, and registration. Your foal arrives with complete documentation and verified pedigree.

5
Critical neonatal management — neonatal isoerythrolysis (NI)

Educational note for new ET breeders. When the foal is born, it is alive and healthy. The risk window opens in the first hours of life — at first nursing. If the recipient mare's blood type is incompatible with the foal's, her colostrum (the first milk) contains antibodies that can attack the foal's red blood cells. The foal is not born dead; the danger is the colostrum, not the birth itself. Good ET programs screen recipient mares in advance and, when incompatibility is detected, prevent the foal from nursing the recipient and provide colostrum from a verified safe source — a colostrum bank or a tested compatible mare. With proper screening and management, NI is preventable.

Why ET changes everything for breeders
  • A mare that competes at the NFR can still produce multiple foals that same year — no downtime from pregnancy
  • You don't need to own an elite mare. Access embryos from champion donor mares through our marketplace
  • Frozen embryos travel — source elite genetics from anywhere in the country without shipping a live horse
  • Preserve the bloodline of an aging or deceased elite mare — embryos can be flushed late in her career or banked in advance
  • Multiple crosses per season — test a mare with two different stallions in the same year

Five genes produce
every coat color.

E
Extension
MC1R locus

Controls whether black or red pigment is produced. One dominant E allele = black pigment possible. Two recessive e alleles = red/chestnut base, no black anywhere on the horse.

ASIP locus

Only active when E is present. Restricts black pigment to the points — mane, tail, lower legs — creating bay. Horses with aa express black all over when they carry E.

Cream
MATP locus

One Cr copy dilutes red pigment — sorrel becomes palomino, bay becomes buckskin. Two copies dilute both red and black — sorrel becomes cremello, bay becomes perlino.

TBX3 locus

Washes out the body color while concentrating pigment in primitive markings — dorsal stripe, shoulder stripe, leg barring. Dun on black = grulla. Dun on bay = dun. Dun on sorrel = red dun.

Beginner glossary · Understanding stallion breeding contracts

How stallion breeding contracts actually work.

Most first-time mare owners assume the stallion's owner manages breeding directly. They don't. And many assume one breeding fee equals one dose. It doesn't. Read this before signing anything.

Who actually manages the stallion

Stallions are managed by the stallion station (also called a breeding facility or collection facility) — not by the owner. The station handles collection schedule, semen processing, packaging, and shipment. The owner sets the contract; the station executes the work.

When you book a breeding, ask: where is the stallion physically located, what days does the station collect, and which semen formats are offered (fresh, cooled, frozen).

Contracts update every year

Stallion contracts and semen availability are renegotiated annually. A stallion offered fresh and cooled this year may be frozen-only next year. Stud fees, dose limits, and chute fees can all shift. Always confirm current-year terms — never assume last year's contract still applies.

What "one breeding" actually means

In the AQHA Quarter Horse world, "one breeding" is a guarantee of a live foal — not a guarantee of pregnancy on the first dose. If you pay $2,500 for a breeding and the first dose doesn't take, you typically re-breed under the same contract using additional doses. You keep going until a viable pregnancy is confirmed. The flat breeding fee covers the live foal, not a single shipment.

Common contract terms
  • Live-foal guarantee — a foal that stands and nurses; if not, you re-breed at no additional stud fee (chute/collection fees may still apply).
  • Breeding fee vs stud fee — often used interchangeably, but read the contract — some define stud fee as the booking deposit and breeding fee as the live-foal portion.
  • Dose limits — how many doses you're entitled to under one contract before re-booking.
  • Re-breed policy — what happens if the mare doesn't conceive or loses the pregnancy.
  • Shipped semen vs on-site — on-site usually means cooled fresh and a board fee; shipped means freight + container deposit.
  • Cooled vs frozen protocols — different timing windows and insemination requirements; ask your vet.
Disclaimer: Actual contract terms vary by stallion and station. Ranch 13 does not represent any specific stallion's contract terms. Always read the current-year contract from the station of record and confirm details with your reproductive veterinarian before signing.

AQHA bloodline taxonomy

Not all Quarter Horses
are the same horse.

The single most common expensive mistake in Quarter Horse breeding is mixing bloodline families. A racing-bred stallion will not produce a cutting horse. A ranch-bred mare will not produce a futurity reiner. Understanding the three families — and the sub-lines within them — is the foundation of every breeding decision on this platform.

01
Racing bloodlines
Built for 440 yards

Quarter horse racing is a pure sprint discipline — 220 to 440 yards, straight track, explosive start. These horses are bred for one thing: raw speed in a straight line. Cow sense, collection, and trainability are secondary or absent. Do not cross these into performance programs expecting athletic versatility.

Racing bloodlines crossed into performance programs rarely produce competitive athletes. If you want a barrel racer, look at the Frenchmans Guy or Dash Ta Fame lines — they sit at the intersection of speed and mind. Pure racing crosses almost never work for cow work.

02
Performance bloodlines
Built for the arena

Performance horses compete in timed and judged events that demand cow sense, athleticism, collection, and trainability. This is the largest and most valuable breeding market in AQHA. Sub-lines are highly specialized — a cutting sire and a reining sire may share no meaningful genetics despite both being "performance."

Ranch bloodlines crossed with performance lines can produce outstanding AQHA ranch horse competitors and team penning horses. Foundation blood (King, Leo, Poco Bueno) adds mind and soundness to hot performance crosses — this is an underused strategy on the show circuit.

Racing × Racing

Produces faster racetrack horses. No crossover value. Do not attempt if goal is performance or ranch work.

Performance × Performance

Works well within same discipline. Cutting × Cutting = better cutter. Cutting × Reining = cow horse. Never barrel × cutting.

Ranch × Performance

High potential for ranch horse competitors. Foundation mind + performance athleticism. King or Poco Bueno on a Peptoboonsmal mare is a proven formula.

Racing × Performance

Barrel racing exception only — Dash Ta Fame and Frenchmans Guy crosses can work. All other performance disciplines: avoid this cross entirely.

Breeding fundamentals

Conventional vs ART — which path is right for your mare?Beginner

Conventional breeding is the right choice for most first-time breeders. The mare is bred directly, carries her own foal for ~11 months, and produces one foal per year. ART (embryo transfer, ICSI, OPU) is reserved for high-value mares whose earnings, competition record, or genetic value justify the higher cost. The decision tree: documented competition earnings? Mare needs to stay in the show pen? Goal is multiple foals from one season? → ART. Otherwise → conventional.

The breeding calendar — what happens in each month?Beginner

The traditional Quarter Horse breeding season opens February 1 and runs through July. Mares are typically synchronized in late winter, bred Feb–Apr, ultrasounded at days 7 and 11–12 to confirm pregnancy, and foal out the following Feb–May. ART programs flush in December and January to produce earlier-born foals, which can be a competitive advantage in age-class events.

Neonatal isoerythrolysis (NI) — what it is and how to prevent itCritical · ART

NI is a blood-type incompatibility risk that occurs when a recipient mare's colostrum contains antibodies that destroy the foal's red blood cells. The foal is born alive and healthy — the danger window opens at first nursing. Good ET programs screen recipients in advance for blood-type compatibility and, when incompatibility is detected, prevent the foal from nursing the recipient and provide colostrum from a verified safe source (a colostrum bank or tested compatible mare). With proper screening and management, NI is preventable.

Foal health — first 24 hours, first month, first yearBeginner

First 24 hours: nursing within 1–2 hours, IgG check at 12–24 hours, meconium passage, normal urination. First month: monitor limb conformation, foal-heat scour around days 7–10, mare's milk supply. First year: vaccinations starting around 4–6 months, weaning at 4–6 months, halter breaking, gentle ground work.

Choosing semen — fresh, cooled, frozen, or live cover?Beginner

Fresh = same-day, on-site only. Cooled = shipped overnight, used within 24–48 hours, most flexible for distance. Frozen = stored indefinitely, requires precise timing and a strong AI vet — lower per-cycle conception rate. Live cover = mare and stallion at the same facility, highest conception rates but logistically restrictive.

Synchronizing the mare — how and whyIntermediate

Synchronization brings the mare to a predictable pre-ovulatory state so insemination can be timed precisely. Common protocols use prostaglandin to lyse a corpus luteum, followed by hCG or deslorelin to induce ovulation. A reproductive vet ultrasounds follicles to time the breeding within the optimal 24–36 hour window pre-ovulation.

Genetics & color

The five-locus color model — E, A, Cr, D, RnBeginner

All eight major AQHA coat colors are produced by stacking five genes: Extension (E) — black vs red base; Agouti (A) — restricts black to points (bay); Cream (Cr) — dilutes red and black; Dun (D) — dorsal stripe + body wash; Roan (Rn) — white hairs through body. UC Davis offers the standard panel test that types all five.

5-panel disease screening — HYPP, HERDA, GBED, MH, PSSM1Critical

The AQHA 5-panel screens for the five most common heritable disorders in the breed. HYPP (muscular paralysis episodes), HERDA (skin disorder), GBED (lethal foal storage disease), MH (anesthesia reaction), PSSM1 (muscle myopathy). All breeding stock should be 5-panel tested. Some carrier-status crosses are still permitted; double-positive crosses are not advisable.

Inbreeding coefficient (COI) — what it means for your foalIntermediate

COI measures the probability that two alleles at any locus are identical by descent. A 0% COI means the parents share no recent ancestors; 12.5% is a half-sibling cross; 25% is a full-sibling or parent-offspring cross. Most performance breeders target COI under 6.25%. Linebreeding (concentrating a desired ancestor) raises COI; outcrossing dilutes it. The Ranch 13 matchmaker surfaces COI projections for every cross.

Magic crosses — how the database worksIntermediate

A "magic cross" is a stallion-by-mare-line combination with a documented track record of producing competitive foals. Success rates in the database are calculated from published competition records (NCHA, NRHA, NRCHA, WPRA earnings) and breeder-reported outcomes. Subscribers can submit feedback to refine the data — the database is community-informed and self-correcting over time.

Color outcome probabilities — palomino, grulla, buckskinBeginner

Palomino requires sorrel base (ee) plus one cream gene. Cremello requires sorrel base plus two cream genes (CrCr). Grulla requires black base (E_, aa) plus one dun gene. To produce palomino reliably, breed a homozygous-cream sire (CrCr) to a sorrel mare — every foal carries one Cr. Use the UC Davis panel mode in the simulator for exact probabilities.

AQHA & registration

AQHA membership types — General, Amateur, YouthBeginner

General ($65/yr) — for anyone who owns a Quarter Horse or shows in the Open division. Amateur ($75/yr) — must never have been paid to show, train, or judge. Youth ($25/yr) — under 19. Lifetime memberships available for General and Youth. You have 60 days to switch types if no horse work has been processed under the membership.

Registering a foal — what's requiredBeginner

You need: (1) breeder's certificate from the stallion station, (2) DNA / parentage verification kit submitted to AQHA, (3) registration application with the foal's name, color, markings, and date of birth, (4) registration fee. Apply within the foal's first year for the lower fee. Late registration after 3 years incurs much higher fees.

DNA verification and parentageIntermediate

AQHA requires DNA on file for both sire and dam to register a foal. The foal is hair-sampled and tested against parents to confirm parentage. Most stallion stations require DNA on file before issuing a breeder's certificate. Submit DNA kits well before the breeding to avoid delays at registration time.

AQHA performance points and Records of AchievementIntermediate

AQHA tracks performance points across show classes. A horse earning 10 points in a single class becomes a Register of Merit (ROM) holder. Higher-tier achievements include AQHA Champion, Superior Performance, and World Show qualification. Points stay attached to the horse for life and become part of the genetic record breeders evaluate.

Breed shows vs futurities vs derbiesIntermediate

AQHA breed shows offer halter, ranch riding, all-around, and discipline-specific classes for any registered Quarter Horse. Futurities are age-restricted (typically 3-year-olds) — horses must be nominated, sometimes at conception. Derbies follow futurities and admit 4–6 year-olds. Discipline-specific bodies (NCHA, NRHA, NRCHA, WPRA, PRCA) sanction their own futurities and derbies.

Business of breeding

Understanding stallion contracts — what one breeding meansBeginner

"One breeding" guarantees a live foal — not a single dose. If the first dose doesn't take, you re-breed under the same flat fee until a viable pregnancy is confirmed. Read the contract: live-foal guarantee terms, dose limits, chute fees, shipping fees, and cooled vs frozen protocols all vary by stallion and station. Contracts update annually — last year's terms don't apply this year.

Stallion stations vs stallion ownersBeginner

Stallions are managed by their station (also called a breeding facility or collection facility), not by the owner directly. The owner sets contract terms; the station handles physical collection days, semen processing, packaging, and shipment. When booking, ask: where is the stallion physically housed, what days does the station collect, and which formats are offered (fresh, cooled, frozen).

Cooled vs frozen semen — logistics and protocolsIntermediate

Cooled semen ships overnight in a temperature-controlled container and must be used within 24–48 hours of collection. Container deposits are typical. Frozen semen ships in liquid nitrogen tanks, stays viable indefinitely, but requires a more skilled AI vet, post-thaw motility evaluation, and tighter timing relative to ovulation. Conception rates per dose are typically lower with frozen.

Selling embryos — pricing, contracts, AQHA paperworkAdvanced

Embryos can be sold fresh (same-cycle transfer) or frozen for later use. Pricing varies wildly with the donor mare's earnings and the stallion's reputation — $5K–$60K+ per embryo. AQHA paperwork: the breeder of record is the donor mare's owner at the time of conception. Both donor and stallion must have DNA on file. Contracts should specify guarantees (pregnancy, live foal, recipient management) and ownership transfer points.

Market valuation — what's a stallion's stud fee actually worth?Advanced

Stud fees track three signals: the stallion's own competition earnings, his offspring's earnings (progeny earnings), and demand (book size + waitlist). HorseFax surfaces all three plus comparable foal sale prices over the past 24 months. A $2,500 stud fee for a stallion with $40M+ in offspring earnings is materially different from a $2,500 fee for a stallion with no progeny record.

ROI strategy — when does ART pay for itself?Advanced

ART typically breaks even when the donor mare has documented earnings of $50K+ or her foals consistently sell at $20K+. Selling 2–3 embryos per season at $8K–$15K typically covers clinic, recipient, and stud fees. Add the option value of keeping the donor in competition (where her earnings continue) and ART starts to clearly outperform conventional for the right mare.

Why competition matters to breeders

Shows test the genetics

Shows aren't just for prize money. They are real-world performance tests of progeny traits. When a stallion's offspring consistently win in their discipline, that validates expected progeny values. When they don't, the cross has just disclosed real information. Watching a sire's offspring compete is one of the most reliable ways to evaluate his breeding value.

Never registered with the American Quarter Horse Association? This guide walks you through every step — from choosing the right membership to getting your horse's papers in hand. Plain English. No prior experience needed.

Steps 2–8 · Complete walkthrough
2
Go to AQHA.com and create an account

Visit aqha.com and click "Join AQHA" at the top of the page. You'll create a username and password. Have your personal information ready — full legal name, mailing address, email, and phone number.

Tip: Use the email you check regularly. All horse papers, confirmations, and renewal notices go here.
3
Select your membership type and pay

Choose General, Amateur, or Youth based on the guide above. Pay online by credit card. You'll receive a membership ID number — write this down. You'll need it for everything going forward.

Remember: You have 60 days to change your membership type for free, as long as you haven't processed any horse paperwork yet.
4
Create your myAQHA portal account

Go to myaqha.com — this is AQHA's new member portal where you manage everything: registrations, transfers, DNA orders, show records, and ownership history. It replaced the old Member Services section. Make sure your phone and email are current before you create the account.

Note: myAQHA is free — it's just the online hub for your membership. You still pay separately for registrations and other services.
5
Gather your horse's information

Before you can register a foal, you need these documents and facts confirmed:

The sire's AQHA registration number and proof that he is DNA typed with AQHA
The dam's AQHA registration number and proof that she is DNA typed with AQHA
A completed Stallion Breeding Report on file (the stallion owner files this by November 30 of the breeding year)
The foal's date of birth, gender, color, and all white markings noted
Five photos: left side, right side, front, back, and three-quarter angle
Both parents must be registered AQHA horses (or the sire may be a Jockey Club-registered Thoroughbred). If either parent is not registered, the foal cannot be registered.
6
Submit the registration application online

Log into myAQHA.com, click "Registration" under Ownership, and select your mare to begin. The online application is faster and $5 cheaper than mailing a paper form. Online applications are also given processing priority.

Enter the stallion breeding report, breeding dates, and breeding method
Choose a name for your horse (check availability — names must be unique)
Select color and gender from the dropdown lists
Enter all white markings using the markings diagram tool
Upload all five required photos
Add to cart and pay — fees are based on the horse's age at submission
7
Order the DNA kit and submit hair sample

If your foal was produced by transported semen, frozen semen, or embryo transfer, a DNA kit is required. Order it during the registration process. AQHA will email you a DNA submission form — print it, pull 30–50 hairs from the mane or tail (with roots attached), tape them to the form, and mail it to the lab.

Processing time: DNA results typically take 10–14 business days at the lab. If you need papers fast, you can pay a $100 Rush Fee — but don't rush if DNA testing is required, since the DNA results are the bottleneck.
8
Receive your papers and set up auto-renewal

Once AQHA processes your registration, you'll receive your horse's official certificate of registration — the "papers." Store the original in a safe place and keep a copy with your horse records. Your membership will auto-renew annually unless you opt out. You'll be notified before each renewal.

Questions? Call AQHA Member Services at 806-376-4811, Monday–Friday 8 a.m.–5 p.m. Central. They are genuinely helpful.

Common questions
My horse doesn't have papers. Can I still register it? +
Only if both parents are registered AQHA horses or a Jockey Club-registered Thoroughbred. If neither parent is registered, the horse cannot be registered as an AQHA Quarter Horse. If you own the horse and want to participate in AQHA events, contact AQHA about Appendix Registry options for horses with one Thoroughbred parent.
I bought a horse that's already registered. How do I transfer it to my name? +
You need your AQHA membership ID, the horse's registration certificate (papers), and the seller's signature on the transfer section. Log into myAQHA.com, click "Transfer" under Ownership, enter the horse's registration number and the seller's information, and pay the transfer fee. Keep a copy of the original papers before mailing them to AQHA.
How long does registration take? +
Online applications are processed faster than mail. If no DNA testing is required, most registrations are completed in a few business days. If DNA testing is required, add 10–14 business days for the lab to process the hair sample. A $100 Rush Fee speeds up the AQHA processing side but does not accelerate the DNA lab.
What is a Stallion Breeding Report and who files it? +
The stallion owner files a Stallion Breeding Report with AQHA by November 30 each year, listing every mare the stallion was exposed to. The filing fee is $50 plus $6 per mare. Once filed, AQHA sends pre-printed registration applications (breeder's certificates) to the stallion owner to distribute to mare owners. Without this report on file, your foal's registration cannot be completed.
I chose the wrong membership type. Can I change it? +
Yes — you have 60 days from the date AQHA receives your membership application to change or cancel it, as long as no horse-related work (registrations, transfers, DNA tests) has been processed under that membership. After that window, or if any work has been done, changes are not possible. Call AQHA at 806-376-4811.
